While PNG is excellent for graphics and transparency, it produces large files for photographs, where JPG is far more efficient. Converting PNG to JPG is the right move when you have a photo saved as PNG and want a smaller, more web-friendly file without meaningful quality loss.

PNG Versus JPG, and What Changes

PNG is lossless and supports transparency, which makes it ideal for graphics but results in large files — especially for photographs, where its lossless approach cannot compress the rich detail efficiently. JPG uses lossy compression tuned for photographic content, producing dramatically smaller files with quality loss that is usually invisible in a photo. So for a photographic image, converting PNG to JPG can shrink the file enormously while looking essentially the same.

Two things change in the conversion worth knowing. First, JPG cannot store transparency, so any transparent areas in the PNG will be filled in — typically with a solid background color — when converted. This is fine for photos but means you should not convert a logo or graphic that relies on transparency. Second, JPG's compression is lossy, so the conversion does introduce some quality reduction; for photographs this is generally imperceptible at good quality settings, which is exactly the trade JPG is designed for.

When to Convert PNG to JPG

The classic case is a photograph saved as PNG. Screenshots of photos, images exported from certain tools, or photos saved in PNG by default can be many times larger than they need to be. Converting to JPG cuts the file size dramatically with no visible quality loss, which matters for web pages (faster loading), email attachments (size limits), and storage. If you have a large PNG that is actually a photo, converting to JPG is almost always worthwhile.

The key is knowing when NOT to convert. Because JPG loses transparency and is lossy, it is the wrong choice for graphics, logos, screenshots of text or interfaces, and anything with sharp edges or transparent backgrounds — these belong in PNG, where they stay crisp and can keep transparency. So the rule of thumb is: convert PNG to JPG for photographic content where file size matters, but keep graphics, logos, and text-heavy images as PNG. Matching the format to the content is what gives you the best combination of quality and file size.

Quick Tips

  • Convert photos saved as PNG to JPG for a much smaller file with no visible quality loss.
  • JPG cannot keep transparency — transparent areas get filled with a background color.
  • Do not convert logos, graphics, or text images to JPG; keep those as PNG to stay crisp.
  • Match format to content: JPG for photos, PNG for graphics and transparency.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why convert PNG to JPG?

Mainly to reduce file size. For photographic content, JPG produces dramatically smaller files than PNG with no visible quality loss, making images faster to load and easier to send.

What happens to transparency when converting to JPG?

JPG cannot store transparency, so transparent areas are filled in, usually with a solid background color. For this reason, do not convert images that rely on transparency, like logos.

Does converting to JPG reduce quality?

JPG is lossy, so the conversion introduces some compression. For photographs at good quality settings this is generally imperceptible, which is what JPG is designed for. For graphics and text, the loss is more noticeable.

Are my images uploaded anywhere?

No. The conversion happens entirely in your browser, so your images never leave your device. It is both private and fast.
