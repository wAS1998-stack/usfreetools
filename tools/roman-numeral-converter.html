How Roman Numerals Work

Roman numerals use seven letters, each with a value: I is 1, V is 5, X is 10, L is 50, C is 100, D is 500, and M is 1,000. Numbers are built by combining these, generally from largest to smallest, adding the values together — so XV is 15 (10 + 5) and CCC is 300. The system has no zero and no place value in the way our decimal system does; it is purely additive, with one important twist.

That twist is subtractive notation, which keeps numerals from getting too long. When a smaller value sits before a larger one, it is subtracted rather than added: IV is 4 (5 − 1) rather than IIII, IX is 9, XL is 40, and CM is 900. This is why a year like 1994 becomes MCMXCIV — M (1000), CM (900), XC (90), IV (4). The rules about which subtractions are allowed are specific, and getting them right by hand is fiddly, which is exactly what makes an automatic converter handy.

Where Roman Numerals Are Used

Roman numerals persist in a surprising number of places. Clock and watch faces often use them, lending a classic look. Books use them for introductory page numbers, chapter titles, and volume numbers. Films and television famously write their copyright year in Roman numerals in the credits, which is why people reach for a converter to decode them. Outlines and lists use them to mark major sections. And monuments, cornerstones, and official inscriptions frequently record dates in Roman numerals for their timeless, formal character.

They are also the traditional way to number things in a series of people or events sharing a name — monarchs (Elizabeth II), popes, and sequels all use Roman numerals to indicate succession. Super Bowls are numbered this way, as are many recurring events and editions. Because the system carries a sense of tradition and formality, it is chosen deliberately where that tone is wanted. For all these everyday encounters — decoding a date in the credits, numbering an outline, or writing a year in formal style — a converter turns an obscure skill into an instant lookup.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do Roman numerals work?

Seven letters carry values — I, V, X, L, C, D, M — and are combined, generally largest to smallest, adding up. The exception is subtractive notation, where a smaller numeral before a larger one is subtracted, as in IV for 4.

What is subtractive notation?

It is the rule that a smaller value placed before a larger one is subtracted, keeping numerals shorter. IV is 4 (not IIII), IX is 9, XL is 40, and CM is 900. The converter applies these rules automatically.

What is MCMXCIV?

It is 1994: M (1000) + CM (900) + XC (90) + IV (4). Reading it requires applying the subtractive rules, which is exactly the kind of thing this converter does instantly.

Do Roman numerals have a zero?

No. The Roman system has no symbol for zero and no place value like our decimal system. It is an additive system with subtractive exceptions, which is part of why large numbers can look complex.
