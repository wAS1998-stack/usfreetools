🔡 Character Counter

The character counter tells you exactly how many characters are in your text, with and without spaces. This is essential for platforms with strict limits like Twitter/X (280), meta descriptions (160), and SMS messages (160).

What This Tool Does

This tool counts the characters in your text in real time as you type or paste — both with and without spaces — so you always know exactly where you stand against a limit. It also tracks words, sentences, and paragraphs, giving you a complete picture at a glance. Whenever something you are writing has a hard character limit, this is the fastest way to stay within it.

Character limits are everywhere: social media posts, meta descriptions, SMS messages, form fields, bios, and headlines. Hitting one mid-sentence and watching your text truncate is frustrating and avoidable. Counting as you write means you can shape your message to fit rather than discovering the problem after you publish.

Why Both With and Without Spaces Matter

It might seem odd to count characters two ways, but the distinction is genuinely important because different systems count differently. Most social platforms and SMS include spaces in their limits, so the with-spaces total is what you measure against. Some writing-volume requirements, by contrast, look at characters excluding spaces. Having both numbers means you are never guessing which one a particular limit refers to.

Spaces add up faster than people expect — a 280-character tweet can contain forty or more spaces. That is why eyeballing length is unreliable and an accurate counter matters. The tool shows both figures side by side so you can match whichever one your situation requires without doing mental arithmetic.

Character Limits Worth Knowing

A handful of limits come up constantly. Standard posts on X cap at 280 characters. A search engine meta description displays cleanly up to roughly 155 to 160 characters before being cut off. A single SMS message is 160 characters, dropping to 70 the moment you add an emoji that shifts the encoding. Instagram captions allow far more, but only the first 125 or so show before the “more” cutoff. Knowing these means you can write to fit from the start.

The trickier part is that emoji and some special characters can count as more than one character because of how text is encoded. An emoji-heavy message reaches a limit faster than its visible length suggests. When you are close to a cap, this counter reflects the real character usage, so you can trim with confidence rather than hoping you have enough room.

Quick Tips

  • Watch the with-spaces total for social media and SMS limits — that is what those platforms measure.
  • Front-load your most important words, so the message still lands even if it gets cut off.
  • Emoji can quietly eat into your limit faster than expected — trim them first if you are over.
  • For meta descriptions, aim for about 155 characters so nothing gets truncated in search results.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do spaces count as characters?

On most platforms, yes — spaces count toward the limit, which is why the with-spaces total is usually the one to watch. This tool shows both totals so you can match whichever your situation uses.

Why might an emoji count as more than one character?

Because of how text is encoded, some emoji are represented by more than one underlying unit, so they can consume two or more characters against a limit. An emoji-heavy message hits a cap sooner than its visible length suggests.

What is the character limit for a tweet?

Standard accounts on X have a 280-character limit. Note that links are automatically counted as a fixed length regardless of their real length, and some emoji count as more than one character.

Is my text private?

Yes. All counting happens in your browser and nothing is uploaded, so you can paste private or sensitive text safely.
