JPG and PNG are the two most common image formats on the web, each with real strengths. Knowing when to use which, and being able to convert between them quickly, is genuinely useful whenever you work with images for websites, design, or sharing.

JPG Versus PNG

JPG uses lossy compression, making small files that are ideal for photographs, where its slight quality trade-offs are invisible among the rich detail. But it cannot store transparency, and repeated saving degrades quality. PNG uses lossless compression, so it preserves every pixel perfectly, and crucially it supports transparency — areas of the image that are see-through. This makes PNG the right choice for graphics, logos, screenshots, text, and anything with sharp edges or transparent backgrounds.

Converting from JPG to PNG is most useful when you need those PNG strengths. The important thing to understand is that converting a JPG to PNG cannot recover quality the JPG already lost — the lossy compression that happened when the JPG was created is permanent. What you gain is lossless storage from that point on (no further degradation with re-saving) and the ability to add transparency. So the conversion is about format capabilities going forward, not about magically improving an image that was already compressed.

When to Convert JPG to PNG

The most common reason is transparency. JPG simply cannot have a transparent background, so if you need an image to sit on different colored backgrounds, blend into a design, or have a logo without a white box around it, you need PNG. Converting a JPG to PNG is the first step — though note that the conversion alone does not remove the existing background; it just gives you a format that can support transparency once you edit it. For graphics that will be composited or layered, PNG is essential.

The other reason is preserving quality through editing. Because PNG is lossless, it does not degrade each time you save it, unlike JPG which loses a little quality with every re-save. If you are going to edit an image repeatedly, working in PNG avoids that cumulative loss. PNG is also the better choice for images with sharp edges, text, line art, or solid colors, where JPG's compression can create visible artifacts and fuzziness around the crisp parts. For screenshots especially, PNG keeps text and interface elements clean and readable where JPG would blur them.

Quick Tips

  • Convert to PNG when you need transparency or lossless, edit-friendly quality.
  • PNG is best for graphics, logos, screenshots, and anything with sharp edges or text.
  • Converting cannot recover quality a JPG already lost — that compression is permanent.
  • For photographs without transparency needs, JPG's smaller file size is usually preferable.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why convert JPG to PNG?

Mainly for transparency support and lossless quality. PNG can have transparent areas and does not degrade when re-saved, making it better for graphics, logos, screenshots, and images you will edit repeatedly.

Does converting to PNG improve image quality?

No. Converting cannot recover detail the JPG already discarded — that loss is permanent. What you gain is lossless storage going forward and the ability to support transparency.

When should I keep an image as JPG instead?

For photographs without transparency needs, JPG's much smaller file size is usually the better choice. PNG files of photos are far larger with no visible benefit.

Are my images uploaded anywhere?

No. The conversion happens entirely in your browser, so your images never leave your device. It is both private and fast.
