What This Tool Does

This tool converts between units of speed — miles per hour, kilometers per hour, meters per second, knots, and more. Enter a speed in any unit and it gives you the equivalents instantly. It is the quick way to make sense of speeds quoted in unfamiliar units, whether you are driving abroad, reading weather or sports data, or working through a physics problem.

Speed crosses between units constantly: road signs and car speedometers differ by country, wind and weather speeds appear in several units, and science uses meters per second while everyday life uses miles or kilometers per hour. Having all the units in one converter means you can translate any speed into terms you understand without juggling separate factors.

The Common Speed Units

The everyday units are miles per hour (mph), standard in the US and UK, and kilometers per hour (km/h), used across most of the world — the two you meet on roads. The bridge between them is the same as for distance: one mile per hour is about 1.609 kilometers per hour. Meters per second (m/s) is the scientific standard, used in physics and often in weather data. Knots, equal to one nautical mile per hour, are used in aviation and at sea.

Because these units come from different worlds — everyday travel, science, aviation, sailing — converting between them by hand means remembering several unrelated factors. A 60 mph speed is about 97 km/h, roughly 27 m/s, and about 52 knots, and there is no simple pattern linking them. That is exactly why a converter helps: it holds all the relationships at once, so you can move a speed from whichever unit it was given in to whichever one you actually need.

Where Speed Conversion Comes Up

Driving abroad is the most common everyday case. A speed limit sign showing 100 means 100 km/h in most countries, about 62 mph — and a driver used to mph needs that conversion to stay safe and legal. Car speedometers, GPS readouts, and rental-car displays can all be in the unfamiliar unit, so converting helps you judge your speed accurately. Misreading the unit can mean unintentionally speeding or crawling along, so getting it right genuinely matters on the road.

Beyond driving, speed conversion appears in weather, sport, and science. Wind speeds are reported in mph, km/h, m/s, or knots depending on the source, so understanding a forecast often means converting. Aviation and sailing use knots, which most people need to translate into more familiar units. Physics and engineering work in meters per second. Sports report speeds — a serve, a pitch, a sprint — in various units. In all these settings, an all-in-one speed converter turns an unfamiliar number into one you can immediately make sense of.

Quick Tips

  • One mph is about 1.609 km/h — the same factor as miles to kilometers for distance.
  • A 100 km/h limit is about 62 mph; misreading the unit means badly misjudging your speed.
  • Knots (used in aviation and sailing) are nautical miles per hour, a different unit again.
  • Meters per second is the scientific standard, common in physics and weather data.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I convert mph to km/h?

Multiply the mph figure by about 1.609. So 60 mph is about 97 km/h. The converter does this and other speed conversions instantly.

What is a knot?

A knot is one nautical mile per hour, used in aviation and at sea. One knot is about 1.15 mph or 1.85 km/h. It is a different unit from the miles per hour used on roads.

What speed unit does science use?

Meters per second (m/s) is the standard scientific unit, used in physics and often in weather data. The converter translates it to and from everyday units like mph and km/h.

Why does the driving-abroad speed conversion matter?

Because speed limits are posted in km/h in most countries but mph in the US and UK. Misreading a 100 km/h limit as mph would have you driving far too fast, so converting keeps you safe and legal.
