⚖️ BMI Calculator

Calculate your Body Mass Index (BMI) using your height and weight, in either imperial or metric units, and see which BMI category you fall into.

What This Calculator Does

This calculator works out your Body Mass Index (BMI) from your height and weight, in either metric or imperial units, and tells you which standard category the result falls into. BMI is a widely used screening number that gives a quick, rough sense of whether your weight is in a healthy range for your height. Enter your measurements and it does the calculation and interprets the result instantly.

BMI is popular because it is simple and gives a fast, standardized snapshot. It is important to understand from the outset, though, that it is a general screening tool, not a measure of health or body composition for any individual — a point worth keeping in mind as you read your result. This calculator gives you the number and its category; the context below explains what that does and does not mean.

How BMI Is Calculated and Categorized

BMI is calculated from your weight relative to your height — specifically, weight divided by height squared. The resulting number is then placed into standard categories: roughly, under 18.5 is considered underweight, 18.5 to 24.9 is the normal or healthy range, 25 to 29.9 is overweight, and 30 and above is in the obese range. These thresholds are the widely used standard, which is why BMI gives a consistent, comparable figure across people.

The calculation works the same regardless of units — you can enter height and weight in metric (centimeters and kilograms) or imperial (feet, inches, and pounds), and the calculator handles the conversion. The single resulting number is what makes BMI convenient: it reduces the relationship between height and weight to one figure that fits into clear categories. That simplicity is its strength as a quick screening tool, and also, as the next section explains, the source of its well-known limitations.

Understanding BMI and Its Limits

BMI is genuinely useful as a quick, population-level screening tool. For large groups, it correlates reasonably well with body fat and with health risks associated with being significantly under- or overweight, which is why health organizations use it. For an individual, it can be a helpful starting point — a rough flag that might prompt a conversation with a healthcare provider, or a simple way to track a general trend over time. As a first, broad indicator, it has real value precisely because it is so easy to calculate and standardized.

But BMI has important limitations that everyone should know. It does not distinguish between muscle and fat, so very muscular people can register as overweight despite being lean and healthy. It does not account for where fat is carried, for age, sex, or ethnicity, all of which affect what a given BMI means. An athlete and a sedentary person can share a BMI while having very different health profiles. For these reasons, BMI should be treated as one rough screening number among many, not a diagnosis or a verdict on your health. This calculator is for general informational purposes and is not medical advice — for a genuine assessment of your health, a healthcare professional can consider BMI alongside the many other factors that actually matter.

Quick Tips

Frequently Asked Questions

How is BMI calculated?

It is your weight divided by your height squared. The calculator accepts metric or imperial units and produces a single number, then places it in the standard categories: underweight, normal, overweight, or obese.

What is a healthy BMI range?

The widely used standard considers 18.5 to 24.9 the normal or healthy range, with under 18.5 underweight, 25 to 29.9 overweight, and 30 and above obese. These are general thresholds, not individual judgments.

Is BMI an accurate measure of health?

Not on its own. BMI is a rough screening tool that does not distinguish muscle from fat or account for age, sex, or where fat is carried. It is a starting point, not a diagnosis or a measure of individual health.

Should I be concerned about my BMI?

BMI can flag a possible concern worth discussing, but it is not a verdict. A healthcare professional can assess your health properly, considering BMI alongside the many other factors it does not capture. This tool is informational, not medical advice.
