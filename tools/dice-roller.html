🎲 Online Dice Roller

Roll one or many virtual dice online with fair, random results — perfect for board games and tabletop RPGs.

What This Tool Does

This tool rolls virtual dice for you — any number of dice, with any number of sides — and shows the result instantly. Whether you need a quick six-sided roll for a board game, a twenty-sided die for tabletop role-playing, or a fistful of dice at once, it handles it without anyone having to find physical dice or worry about one rolling off the table. The results are random, so every roll is fair.

It is handy any time you need a random outcome and do not have dice to hand: settling a decision, running a game night when the dice box has gone missing, or playing a tabletop game on the move. Because it lives in your browser, it is always available on whatever device is nearest.

How the Randomness Works

A good dice roller has to be genuinely unpredictable, and this one generates each result using your browser's random number facilities so that every face has an equal chance of coming up. There is no pattern, no memory of previous rolls, and no bias toward any number — just as a fair physical die has no memory and no preference. Each roll stands entirely on its own.

This matters because people often misread randomness. After three sixes in a row, a fair die is exactly as likely to roll a six on the next throw as any other number — the dice do not “know” what came before. A virtual roller makes this concrete: streaks happen by chance, and they do not change the odds of the next roll. Understanding that is the difference between reading randomness correctly and falling for the gambler's fallacy.

Dice in Games, From Board Games to Tabletop RPGs

Different games lean on different dice. Classic board games mostly use the familiar six-sided die, sometimes two at a time, where rolling doubles or hitting a particular total drives the action. Tabletop role-playing games go much further, using a whole family of dice — four, six, eight, ten, twelve, and twenty sides — each suited to different outcomes. The twenty-sided die in particular is iconic, used to resolve actions where a wide range of results keeps things tense and unpredictable.

Rolling several dice at once changes the feel of the result in an interesting way. A single die gives every outcome an equal chance, but rolling two and adding them clusters results toward the middle — a total of seven is far more likely than two or twelve, because more combinations produce it. This is why many games use multiple dice: it creates a natural bell curve where average results are common and extremes are rare, which often makes for better gameplay. This roller lets you set the number and type of dice, so you can match whatever your game calls for.

Quick Tips

  • Set both the number of dice and the number of sides to match your specific game.
  • Remember that past rolls do not influence future ones — a fair die has no memory.
  • Rolling multiple dice and summing them clusters results toward the middle values.
  • For a simple yes/no or pick-between-two decision, a single die is the fastest fair method.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are the dice rolls truly random?

Yes. Each roll uses your browser's random number generation, giving every face an equal chance with no pattern or bias. Every roll is independent of the ones before it, just like fair physical dice.

If I roll several sixes in a row, is a six less likely next time?

No. Each roll is independent, so a fair die is exactly as likely to show a six after three sixes as at any other time. Believing otherwise is the gambler's fallacy — the dice have no memory.

Can I roll different kinds of dice?

Yes. You can choose the number of sides — four, six, eight, ten, twelve, twenty, or more — and the number of dice, so it works for board games and tabletop role-playing games alike.

Why does rolling two dice favor the middle numbers?

Because more combinations add up to middle totals. With two six-sided dice, a total of seven can be made several ways while two or twelve can each be made only one way, so middle totals come up more often.
