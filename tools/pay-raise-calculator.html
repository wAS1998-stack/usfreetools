How a Raise Is Calculated

The math is a direct percentage calculation. Your annual increase equals your current salary multiplied by the raise percentage divided by 100. Your new salary is simply your current salary plus that increase, or equivalently your current salary multiplied by one plus the raise as a decimal. The extra per month is the annual increase divided by twelve. For example, a 5% raise on a $50,000 salary adds $2,500 per year — a new salary of $52,500, or about $208 more each month before taxes. Remember that these are gross figures; your take-home increase will be somewhat smaller after taxes and deductions.

Worked Examples

A standard annual raise

On a $60,000 salary, a 4% raise adds $2,400 per year, bringing you to $62,400, or about $200 more per month before tax. Seeing the monthly figure helps you decide how much of the raise to save versus spend.

Comparing two offers

One job offers a 6% raise on your current $70,000, another offers a flat $5,000 increase. The percentage raise is $4,200, so the flat $5,000 offer is actually larger. Converting percentages to dollars makes comparisons like this clear.

Negotiation planning

You currently earn $55,000 and want to target a specific take-home improvement. By testing different percentages, you can see that asking for 8% ($4,400) versus 5% ($2,750) is a $1,650 annual difference — useful context when deciding what number to bring to the conversation.

Real-World Scenarios

Employees use this when an annual review delivers a percentage raise and they want to know the real-world impact on their budget. Job seekers use it to compare offers expressed in different ways — a percentage bump at one company versus a flat increase at another. People preparing for salary negotiations use it to translate their target into a percentage they can ask for, and to understand the gap between different asks. It is also useful for understanding cost-of-living adjustments, which are often quoted as a percentage and can quietly fail to keep pace with actual inflation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Assuming the full raise lands in your pocket — the calculator shows gross figures, and taxes and deductions reduce the take-home amount.
  • Comparing a percentage raise at one job to a flat-dollar raise at another without converting both to the same units.
  • Ignoring the monthly figure, which is often the most useful number for actual budgeting.
  • Forgetting that a raise compounds future raises, since next year's percentage will be calculated on the higher new salary.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate my salary after a raise?

Multiply your current salary by the raise percentage divided by 100 to get the increase, then add it to your current salary. A 5% raise on $50,000 adds $2,500, for a new salary of $52,500. This calculator does it instantly and also shows the monthly impact.

Is the raise amount before or after taxes?

This calculator shows gross figures — the increase before taxes and deductions. Your actual take-home increase will be somewhat smaller, since the additional income is also taxed. The exact reduction depends on your tax situation.

How do I compare a percentage raise to a flat-dollar raise?

Convert the percentage to dollars by applying it to your current salary, then compare the two dollar amounts directly. A 6% raise on $70,000 is $4,200, which you can compare straightforwardly against a flat $5,000 offer.

Does a raise affect future raises?

Yes. Because future percentage raises are calculated on your new, higher salary, each raise compounds. This is why even a modest difference in this year's raise percentage can grow into a meaningful gap over several years.

Methodology & Accuracy

This calculator applies the standard percentage-increase formula: the increase equals current salary times the raise percentage divided by 100, and the new salary is the sum of the two. The monthly figure divides the annual increase by twelve. All values are gross — before taxes and deductions — because that is how raises are typically quoted and negotiated. Calculation happens entirely in your browser. For your actual take-home change, you would need to account for your specific tax bracket and deductions, which this tool does not estimate.
