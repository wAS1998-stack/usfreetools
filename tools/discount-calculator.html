🏷️ Discount Calculator

Calculate the final price after a discount and see exactly how much money you save. Perfect for shopping and sales.

What This Calculator Does

This calculator works out the final price after a discount, and shows you exactly how much you save. Enter the original price and the discount percentage, and it gives you the sale price and the dollar amount off. It is the quickest way to answer the question every shopper asks at a sale rack: what will this actually cost me, and how much am I really saving?

Sale signs are designed to look appealing, but the real number — what you pay at the register — takes a quick calculation that is easy to get slightly wrong in your head. This tool gives you the exact figure instantly, so you can judge a deal clearly and avoid the common trap of assuming a discount is bigger than it is.

How Discounts Are Calculated

A discount is a percentage taken off the original price. To find the amount saved, multiply the original price by the discount percentage as a decimal: 25% off a $80 item is $80 times 0.25, or $20 saved. The sale price is then the original minus the saving — $60 in that example. Equivalently, you can multiply the original by what is left after the discount: 75% of $80 is $60, the same answer in one step.

That one-step method is a useful shortcut. If something is 30% off, you are paying 70% of the price, so multiply by 0.70. If it is 40% off, multiply by 0.60. Thinking in terms of what you pay rather than what you save often makes the mental math faster, and it is exactly how the calculator arrives at the final price for you.

Reading Sales Like a Smart Shopper

Discounts can be presented in ways that make them seem larger than they are, so a little clarity helps. A common one is stacked discounts: an item marked 50% off, then an extra 20% off. These do not add up to 70% off, as it might seem. The second discount applies to the already-reduced price, so 20% off the half-price item brings the total saving to 60%, not 70%. Calculating each step in turn — or simply finding the final price directly — keeps you from overestimating the deal.

It is also worth remembering that a percentage saving means more in dollars on an expensive item than a cheap one. Ten percent off a $500 purchase saves $50, while the same 10% off a $20 item saves just $2. When deciding whether a discount is worth acting on, the dollar amount often matters more than the percentage — and seeing both, as this calculator shows, helps you judge whether a sale is genuinely worth it rather than reacting to an attractive-looking percentage alone.

Quick Tips

  • For the final price in one step, multiply by what you pay — 30% off means multiplying by 0.70.
  • Stacked discounts do not simply add up; the second applies to the already-reduced price.
  • A percentage saving is worth more in dollars on expensive items than cheap ones.
  • Look at the dollar saving, not just the percentage, when deciding if a deal is worth it.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate a discount?

Multiply the original price by the discount percentage as a decimal to find the saving, then subtract it from the original. A 25% discount on $80 saves $20, for a sale price of $60.

What is a quick way to find the sale price?

Multiply the original by what you pay. For 30% off, you pay 70%, so multiply by 0.70. This gives the final price directly in one step.

Do stacked discounts add together?

No. A 50%-off item with an extra 20% off is not 70% off. The second discount applies to the already-reduced price, making the total saving 60%, not 70%.

Does this include sales tax?

No. The calculator shows the discounted price before tax. Sales tax, where it applies, is added to the discounted price separately and varies by location.
